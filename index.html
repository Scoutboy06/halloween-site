The Origins of Halloween

Halloween is a holiday celebrated each year on October 31, and Halloween 2023 will occur on Tuesday, October 31. The tradition originated with the ancient Celtic festival of Samhain, when people would light bonfires and wear costumes to ward off ghosts. In this article, we will explore the origins and evolution of Halloween through different chapters that span from ancient times to the present day.

The Celtic Roots of Halloween

Halloween’s origins date back to the ancient Celtic festival of Samhain (pronounced sow-in, so-ween or saw-wen), the Celtic New Year’s festival. The name means “summer’s end”, and the festival marked the close of the harvest season and the coming of winter. The Celts, who lived 2,000 years ago, mostly in the area that is now Ireland, the United Kingdom and northern France, celebrated their new year on November 11.

This day marked the end of summer and the harvest and the beginning of the dark, cold winter, a time of year that was often associated with human death. Celts believed that on the night before the new year, the boundary between the worlds of the living and the dead became blurred. On the night of October 31 they celebrated Samhain, when it was believed that the ghosts of the dead returned to earth.

To commemorate the event, Druids built huge sacred bonfires, where the people gathered to burn crops and animals as sacrifices to the Celtic deities. During the celebration, the Celts wore costumes, typically consisting of animal heads and skins, and attempted to tell each other’s fortunes1. When the celebration was over, they re-lit their hearth fires, which they had extinguished earlier that evening, from the sacred bonfire to help protect them during the coming winter.

The Celtic festival of Samhain was not only a time of celebrating the end of the harvest and the beginning of winter, but also a time of honoring the ancestors and the spirits of the dead. The Celts believed that the souls of the dead were able to return to their homes and visit their living relatives during Samhain. They also believed that the aos sí, or the fairies, were more active and powerful at this time, and could cause mischief or harm to humans.

To welcome and appease the spirits, the Celts would leave food and drink outside their doors, or set a place for them at the table. They would also light candles or lanterns to guide them on their way. Some people would wear masks or costumes to disguise themselves as one of the aos sí, hoping to avoid being harmed or taken away by them. Others would dress up as animals or monsters to scare away evil spirits.

The Celts also practiced various forms of divination during Samhain, as they believed that this was a time when the veil between the worlds was thin and the future could be revealed. They would use various methods, such as casting nuts into a fire, reading patterns in tea leaves or apple peels, or interpreting dreams or omens. They would also consult with Druids, who were priests and seers in Celtic society, and who had special knowledge and skills in divination.

The Roman Influence on Halloween

Halloween’s origins are not only Celtic, but also Roman. When the Roman Empire conquered most of the Celtic lands in the first century A.D., they brought with them two festivals that had some similarities and influences on the Celtic Samhain.

One of these festivals was Feralia, which took place on February 21, the last day of a nine-day celebration of family and kinship called Parentalia. Feralia was a day to honor and mourn the dead, especially the ancestors. People would visit the graves of their loved ones and offer them food, drinks, flowers, and wreaths. They would also perform rituals and prayers to pacify the restless spirits and ensure their peace in the afterlife.

The other festival was Pomona, which took place on November 1, the first day of winter according to the old Roman calendar. Pomona was a day to celebrate the goddess of fruit and trees, especially apples. People would honor her by offering her fruits, nuts, honey, and wine. They would also adorn their homes and temples with garlands and branches of trees. They would also enjoy games and contests involving fruits, such as apple bobbing.

Feralia and Pomona had some common elements with Samhain, but also some differences. Both Feralia and Samhain were occasions to remember and honor the dead, but they had different dates and customs. Feralia was more solemn and respectful, while Samhain was more festive and playful. Both Pomona and Samhain were occasions to celebrate the harvest and the change of seasons, but they had different focuses and activities. Pomona was more about fruits and trees, while Samhain was more about crops and animals.

Feralia and Pomona also left some traces on Halloween as we know it today. For example, the practice of leaving food and drink for the spirits may have come from Feralia. The symbol of the apple and the game of apple bobbing may have come from Pomona. The date of November 1 may have also been influenced by Pomona.

The Christianization of Halloween

Halloween’s origins are not only Celtic and Roman, but also Christian. In the eighth century, Pope Gregory III established November 1 as a day to honor all the saints and martyrs of the Church, both known and unknown. He also dedicated a chapel in St. Peter’s Basilica to them. This day became known as All Saints’ Day, All Hallows’ Day, or Hallowmas.

All Saints’ Day was a time to celebrate the lives and achievements of the holy men and women who had died for their faith. It was also a time to pray for their intercession and guidance. All Saints’ Day was a solemn and joyful occasion, marked by attending church services, singing hymns, and reading the names of the saints.

The evening before All Saints’ Day was called All Hallows’ Eve, or Halloween. It was a time to prepare for the feast of the next day, but also a time to remember the souls of those who had not yet reached heaven. The Church taught that some souls, after death, had to undergo a period of purification in a place called Purgatory, where they suffered for their sins before being admitted to heaven.

The Church also designated November 2 as All Souls’ Day, a day to pray for the souls in Purgatory and help them attain heaven faster. On All Souls’ Day, people would visit cemeteries and light candles on graves. They would also offer food and drink to the poor, hoping that they would pray for their deceased relatives and friends.

All Saints’ Day and All Souls’ Day were part of a three-day observance called Allhallowtide, which encompassed Halloween as well. Allhallowtide was a time to reflect on death and life, sin and grace, fear and hope. It was also a time to connect with the past, present, and future generations of Christians.

All Saints’ Day and All Souls’ Day also influenced some aspects of Halloween as we know it today. For example, the practice of dressing up in costumes may have come from the medieval custom of wearing masks and disguises on All Hallows’ Eve to mock or imitate the evil spirits. The practice of trick-or-treating may have come from the medieval custom of begging for soul cakes on All Souls’ Day in exchange for prayers for the dead. The practice of carving pumpkins may have come from the Irish custom of making lanterns out of turnips or potatoes to represent the souls in Purgatory or to scare away evil spirits.

The Globalization of Halloween

Halloween’s origins are not only Celtic, Roman, and Christian, but also global. In the past century, Halloween has spread from its homeland in Ireland and Britain to many other countries around the world, especially in North America, Europe, and Asia. However, the way Halloween is celebrated varies from place to place, depending on the local culture, history, and traditions.

In North America, Halloween is one of the most popular and commercialized holidays of the year. It is celebrated on October 31 with costumes, parties, trick-or-treating, pranks, and games. People also decorate their houses with pumpkins, spiders, ghosts, and other spooky symbols. Halloween is seen as a fun and festive occasion for people of all ages.

In Europe, Halloween is a relatively new phenomenon that has gained popularity in the last few decades. It is influenced by both the American and the Celtic versions of the holiday. Some countries, such as Ireland, Scotland, England, and Wales, have a long tradition of celebrating Halloween or similar festivals, such as Samhain or Guy Fawkes Day. Other countries, such as France, Germany, Italy, and Spain, have adopted Halloween more recently as a result of cultural exchange and media influence. In Europe, Halloween is usually celebrated by young people who dress up in costumes and go to parties or clubs. Some also go trick-or-treating or visit haunted attractions.

In Asia, Halloween is also a recent trend that has been introduced by foreigners such as teachers and expats. In places that have many expats or tourists, such as China, Japan, Korea, Singapore, and Thailand, there are usually Halloween events and decorations in malls, hotels, bars, and schools. However, some Asian countries also have their own festivals that honor the dead or celebrate the harvest season. For example, in China and Hong Kong, there is the Hungry Ghost Festival in August or September; in India and Nepal, there is Diwali in October or November; in Japan and Korea, there is Obon in July or August; and in Cambodia and Vietnam, there is Pchum Ben or Tet Trung Nguyen in September or October. These festivals have different customs and meanings than Halloween but share some common themes of remembering ancestors and spirits.
