The History of Halloween

By Elias Wennerlund

Halloween has its roots in the Celtic festival of Samhain, which marked the end of summer and the beginning of winter. The Celts believed that on the night of October 31, the boundary between the living and the dead was thin, and that the spirits of the departed could return to visit their homes and cause mischief. They also thought that this was a good time for divination and prophecy, since the otherworld was closer. To celebrate Samhain, the Celts lit bonfires and wore costumes made of animal skins and heads to ward off evil spirits and honor their ancestors.

When the Romans conquered the Celtic lands, they incorporated some of their own festivals into Samhain. One of them was Feralia, a day in late October when the Romans remembered their dead. Another was Pomona, a festival dedicated to the goddess of fruits and trees, whose symbol was an apple. This may explain why apples are associated with Halloween today.

In the 9th century CE, the Christian church established All Saints' Day on November 1 and All Souls' Day on November 2 to honor all the saints and souls who had died. These days were also known as All Hallows' Day and All Hallows' Eve, which later became Halloween. The church tried to replace the pagan customs of Samhain with Christian ones, but some of them persisted, such as lighting candles and praying for the dead続.

As Christianity spread throughout Europe, so did Halloween traditions. In Ireland, people carved turnips or potatoes into lanterns with scary faces to scare away evil spirits. They also went from house to house asking for food or money in exchange for prayers or songs, a practice called souling. In Scotland, people dressed up in costumes and performed tricks or jokes for treats, a practice called guising続.

When Irish and Scottish immigrants came to America in the 19th century, they brought their Halloween customs with them. They soon discovered that pumpkins were easier to carve than turnips, and thus the jack-o-lantern was born. They also popularized trick-or-treating among children, who would dress up in costumes and go door-to-door asking for candy or other goodies続.

Today, Halloween is celebrated in many countries around the world, with different variations and influences. Some countries have their own festivals that coincide with or are similar to Halloween, such as Mexico's Day of the Dead, China's Tomb Sweeping Day, or Japan's Obon Festival. These festivals also honor the dead and celebrate life in different ways続.

Halloween is more than just a fun holiday for dressing up and eating candy. It is a reflection of our human desire to understand and connect with the mysteries of life and death, and to celebrate our ancestors and heritage. It is also a time to express our creativity and imagination, and to enjoy the magic of the season.
